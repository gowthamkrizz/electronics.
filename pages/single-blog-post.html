Why your first PCB spin probably needs a DFM pass

Wei-Lin ChaoVP, Manufacturing Engineering · 6 min read
in 𝕏 ⎘

Almost every board we build for the first time arrives with a schematic the design team is confident in and a layout nobody outside that team has stress-tested against a real production line. That gap is where most early-build problems live — not in the logic, but in the handful of footprint and panelization decisions that only show up once a panel is already moving through reflow.

The mismatch that's easy to miss

The most common issue we catch in DFM review is a footprint pad that's technically correct on paper but doesn't account for the paste volume our stencil deposits at production speed. On a prototype build run by hand, it's forgiving. On a panel moving through a ten-zone reflow oven at line speed, a slightly oversized pad on a fine-pitch part is enough to cause solder bridging.

The second most common catch is panelization — boards designed without enough tooling margin at the edges, or without fiducials placed where our vision system expects to find them. Neither is a design flaw exactly. They're just decisions that look fine in a CAD tool and only matter once the board has to move through a physical machine.

A DFM review doesn't second-guess your design. It tells you what your design will do on a real line, before the real line tells you.

What a 48-hour review actually checks

Our process engineers run every incoming Gerber set and BOM through the same checklist: footprint and paste-volume compatibility, panelization and tooling margin, fiducial placement, and component lifecycle status against the BOM. Most reviews turn around in two business days, and most catch at least one issue worth fixing before the first panel goes through the oven.

Key Takeaways

  • Footprint pads that look fine in CAD can cause bridging at production paste volumes.
  • Panelization and fiducial placement matter more than most first-time designers expect.
  • A 48-hour DFM review costs far less than a respun panel.

None of this requires redesigning the board. Most fixes are footprint or panel-margin adjustments that take an afternoon. The point of running DFM before the first build, rather than after a failed one, is that the fix is cheap when it's still just a file.
